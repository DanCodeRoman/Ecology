Ecology & Levels of Ecology

Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their biotic and abiotic environment.

Levels of Ecology

  • Population Ecology: Studies population sizes and changes over time.
  • Community Ecology: Investigates species interactions and assemblages.
  • Ecosystem Ecology: Focuses on energy flow and ecosystem structure.
  • Global Ecology: Examines how local interactions affect global dynamics.

Measuring Population Size

Often it is impractical to count every individual. Instead, scientists use the mark-recapture method:

  1. Capture 1: Capture and mark a subset of the population (let this number be s).
  2. Release the marked individuals and allow them to mix with the unmarked population.
  3. Capture 2: Capture a new sample (total number n) and count the number of recaptured marked individuals (x).

The estimated population size (N) is given by the formula:

N = (s × n) / x

This formula is based on the assumption that the proportion of marked individuals in the second capture reflects that in the whole population.

Quick Quiz

Suppose you mark 40 individuals, and in the second capture, you catch 50 individuals, of which 10 are marked. What is the estimated population size?

Using the formula: N = (40 × 50) / 10 = 200, so the estimated population size is 200.

Population Growth Models

The Simplest Model

The change in population size over time can be written as:

ΔN/Δt = B - D

Where:

  • B: Number of births (or immigration)
  • D: Number of deaths (or emigration)

For scaling across populations, we define per capita rates:

  • Birth rate (b): Offspring per individual per unit time
  • Death rate (d): Deaths per individual per unit time

This leads to the per capita growth rate, r, where:

r = b - d

Thus, the exponential growth model is represented as:

ΔN/Δt = rN

Interactive Exercise

If a population of 2,500 marmots produces 315 babies and loses 22 adults in one year, calculate r.

r = (315 - 22) / 2500 ≈ 0.1172 per year

Logistic Growth

When resources are limited, population growth slows as it approaches the environment's carrying capacity (K). In the logistic model, birth rates decline and death rates increase as N nears K.

This transition from exponential to logistic growth is key to understanding natural population dynamics.

Survivorship Curves

Survivorship is the proportion of individuals surviving to a given age. It is calculated as:

Survivorship = (Number of individuals surviving to age) / (Total individuals)

Three major types of survivorship curves are often described:

  • Type I: High survival through most of life with a steep decline in later years (e.g., many mammals including humans).
  • Type II: A relatively constant rate of mortality throughout life (e.g., some birds and reptiles).
  • Type III: High mortality early in life with survivors living much longer (e.g., many fish and invertebrates).

Review Question

Which survivorship curve would you expect in a species that produces a large number of offspring but provides little parental care?

A Type III survivorship curve.

Human Population Dynamics

Humans have experienced dramatic changes in population size. Key points include:

  • A significant population explosion due to declines in mortality and changes in fertility.
  • The concept of carrying capacity, which suggests there is an upper limit to the population that the environment can sustain.
  • Modern trends in survivorship and quality of life have altered human population dynamics over time.
